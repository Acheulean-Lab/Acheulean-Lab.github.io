Reoriented Strand Board

A new visual language for wood composites.

Objective How can biomaterials like wood fiber composites be redesigned to make them more compelling for architects, designers, and builders? Can an aesthetic shift increase the proliferation of biomaterials by shedding their perception as weak, ugly, cheap?

Concept A new visual language for wood composites. RSB (Re-oriented Strand Board) is a reconsidered wood composite, embedded with an aesthetic grain structure. RSB encourages architects, designers, and makers to celebrate the materiality of wood composites and biomaterials. RSB creates new application for sustainable wood chip composites, reducing use of hardwood or plywood.

RSB is based on Oriented Strand Board (OSB). OSB is an engineered wood panel used in various construction and furniture applications, made by compressing layers of wood strands with adhesive under high temperature and pressure. RSB amends the rough, stochastic surface of OSB with an integrated grain pattern. By redefining the aesthetic characteristics of wood composite RSB creates new opportunities for its application, and sheds the stigma of OSB as cheap and ugly. The consistent grain within RSB is reminiscent of the repeating grain from in plywood sheets.



Research Material exploration: This project explored the integration of Cymatics forms into composite materials. Cymatics is a well documented area of research that examines the visual patterns produced by the resonance of different material plates at different frequencies. Cymatics relies on a device called a Chladni palate: a speaker mounted to a resonant sheet. The experimentation for this project was conducted with a 10 by 10 inch and 30 by 30 inch steel Chladni palate. Various patterns were generated with small wood chips (2mm) medium (6mm) and full scale OSB strands (2-3.5cm). Large scale (30x30) patterns were documented and used to digitally simulate RSB. Small scale (10x10) patterns were pressed bonded into sheets with wood adhesive to simulate how this material could be mass manufactured and made structural. The vibrated forms (simplified due to the use of the small scale test plate) are emphasized through natural dye, applied to material before vibration and bonding. To create panels a pattern was generated then adhesive and wood material was poured on top, and the entire mix was pressed until solid. Future research will explore full scale 4 by 4 foot panels, and the application of Cymatic aesthetic patterns to other biomaterials.
