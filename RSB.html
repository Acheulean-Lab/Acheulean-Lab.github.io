Reoriented Strand Board

A new visual language for wood composites.

Objective How might wood composites be redesigned to make them more compelling to architects, designers, and builders?

Insight This project was dependent on the observation that the random disorder of particles which defines the aesthetic of wood composites (OSB, Chipboard, MDF, etc.) lead to a perception of the material as weak, ugly, and cheap. Plywood, another form of wood composite, does not share this reputation. Because of the solid veneer across its surface, plywood is visually similar to solid wood, and therefore benefits from an association with strength, craftsmanship, and durability. Can an aesthetic shift toward a more hardwood-like appearance shed wood composite’s perception as an undesirable material?

Concept RSB (Re-oriented Strand Board) is a reconsidered wood composite, embedded with an aesthetic grain structure. RSB is a wood composite that retains its woodiness, a new visual language for wood composites. RSB encourages architects, designers, and makers to celebrate the unique materiality of wood composites, in the same way they might hardwood. RSB creates new application for sustainable wood chip composites, reducing use of solid timber.

RSB is based on Oriented Strand Board (OSB). OSB is an engineered wood panel used in various construction and furniture applications, manufactured by compressing wood chips together with adhesive under high temperature and pressure. RSB amends the stochastic, disarrayed surface of OSB with an integrated grain pattern, formed of dyed wood chips. The grain in RSB is assembled with an automated process suitable for mass manufacture.



Research Material exploration: This project explored the integration of Cymatic forms into composite materials. Cymatics is an area of research that examines waves and their visual expressions. A Chladni Plate vibrates a resonant sheet at various frequencies, scattering particles across the plate surface to visualize resonant frequencies. To create RSB panels a pattern was generated with the Chladni plate, then adhesive and wood material was poured on top, and the entire mix was pressed until solid.

The experiments for this project were conducted with a 10 by 10 inch and 30 by 30 inch steel Chladni palate. Various patterns were generated with small wood chips (2mm) medium (6mm) and full scale OSB strands (2-3.5cm). Large scale (30x30) patterns were documented and used to digitally simulate RSB. Small scale (10x10) patterns were pressed bonded into sheets with wood adhesive to simulate how this material could be mass manufactured and made structural. The vibrated forms (simplified due to the use of the small scale test plate) are emphasized through natural dye, applied to material before vibration and bonding. Future research will explore full scale 4 by 4 foot panels, and the application of Cymatic aesthetic patterns to other biomaterials.
